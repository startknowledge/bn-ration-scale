Rating Scales to Inform Balance Exercise Difficulty during Rehabilitation for Individuals with Neurological Disorders



Rehabilitation for individuals with neurological disorders, such as stroke, spinal cord injuries, or Parkinson's disease, is a complex and challenging process. According to recent statistics, approximately 1 in 4 adults in the United States will experience a neurological disorder during their lifetime (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 2020). Furthermore, a study published in the journal Neurology found that individuals with neurological disorders are at a higher risk of falls and balance-related injuries (Hyndman et al., 2019). Therefore, it is essential to develop effective rehabilitation strategies that address balance exercise difficulty and promote overall physical function.



Introduction to Rating Scales



Rating scales are a crucial tool in rehabilitation, allowing healthcare professionals to assess and monitor an individual's progress, set realistic goals, and tailor exercises to their specific needs. In the context of balance exercise difficulty, rating scales can help inform the level of challenge and intensity of exercises, ensuring that individuals with neurological disorders are neither under- nor over-challenged. This is particularly important, as excessive challenge can lead to frustration and decreased motivation, while insufficient challenge can result in plateaus and lack of progress.



8–10 Actionable Strategies or Tips for Using Rating Scales



Here are 10 actionable strategies or tips for using rating scales to inform balance exercise difficulty during rehabilitation for individuals with neurological disorders:




  • Use a standardized rating scale: Utilize a widely recognized and validated rating scale, such as the Berg Balance Scale (BBS) or the Tinetti Performance-Oriented Mobility Assessment (POMA), to assess balance and mobility.

  • Assess individual needs and goals: Consider the individual's specific needs, goals, and limitations when selecting exercises and determining the level of challenge.

  • Start with simple exercises: Begin with simple exercises that focus on basic balance and mobility skills, such as standing on a firm surface or walking on a level surface.

  • Progress exercises gradually: Gradually increase the level of challenge and intensity of exercises as the individual progresses and demonstrates improved balance and mobility skills.

  • Monitor progress regularly: Regularly assess and monitor the individual's progress, using the rating scale to track changes and adjust exercises accordingly.

  • Use technology to enhance exercises: Consider using technology, such as virtual reality or balance boards, to enhance exercises and provide a more engaging and challenging experience.

  • Focus on functional activities: Incorporate functional activities, such as walking, standing, or reaching, into exercises to promote transfer of skills to everyday life.

  • Encourage patient feedback: Encourage the individual to provide feedback on their experiences and perceptions of exercise difficulty, using this information to adjust exercises and promote a sense of ownership and motivation.

  • Collaborate with interdisciplinary teams: Collaborate with interdisciplinary teams, including physical therapists, occupational therapists, and physicians, to ensure a comprehensive and coordinated approach to rehabilitation.

  • Continuously educate and update knowledge: Stay up-to-date with the latest research and evidence-based practices in rehabilitation, continuously educating oneself on new and innovative approaches to using rating scales and promoting balance exercise difficulty.

Based on this assessment, the physical therapist develops an exercise program that focuses on improving balance and mobility skills, with a gradual progression of exercises to challenge the individual and promote improvement.

  • A 30-year-old individual with a spinal cord injury is assessed using the POMA and found to have a score of 20/28, indicating significant balance and mobility impairment. The occupational therapist uses this information to develop a comprehensive rehabilitation plan that addresses the individual's specific needs and goals, incorporating exercises that promote transfer of skills to everyday life.



Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them



Here are a few common mistakes that can occur when using rating scales to inform balance exercise difficulty during rehabilitation for individuals with neurological disorders, along with tips on how to avoid them:




  • Insufficient assessment: Failing to conduct a comprehensive assessment of the individual's balance and mobility skills can lead to inaccurate or incomplete information. To avoid this, ensure that a thorough assessment is conducted, using a standardized rating scale and considering the individual's specific needs and goals.

  • Inadequate progression of exercises: Failing to gradually progress exercises can result in plateaus and lack of progress. To avoid this, regularly assess and monitor the individual's progress, using the rating scale to track changes and adjust exercises accordingly.

  • Overreliance on technology: Overreliance on technology can lead to a lack of engagement and motivation. To avoid this, incorporate a variety of exercises and activities that promote engagement and motivation, including functional activities and patient-centered approaches.
