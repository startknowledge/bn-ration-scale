How Africa can protect, scale its $97bn FDI gains amid Middle East crisis

As the global economy continues to navigate the challenges posed by the Middle East crisis, Africa has emerged as a significant recipient of Foreign Direct Investment (FDI). According to recent statistics, the continent attracted a record $97 billion in FDI in 2022, with countries such as Egypt, South Africa, and Nigeria leading the charge. This surge in investment is a testament to Africa's growing appeal as a destination for foreign capital, driven by its vast natural resources, growing consumer market, and improving business environment. However, with the ongoing crisis in the Middle East threatening to disrupt global economic stability, Africa must take proactive steps to protect and scale its FDI gains.

Africa's FDI landscape has undergone significant transformations in recent years, with the continent becoming an increasingly important destination for foreign investors. The $97 billion in FDI attracted in 2022 represents a significant increase from the $83 billion recorded in 2020, and a substantial jump from the $50 billion recorded in 2015. This growth in FDI is not limited to a few countries, with many African nations experiencing significant increases in investment. For example, Ethiopia has become a hub for textile manufacturing, with companies such as H&M and Calvin Klein setting up production facilities in the country. Similarly, Rwanda has emerged as a leader in the technology sector, with companies such as Microsoft and Google investing in the country's burgeoning tech industry.

To protect and scale its FDI gains, Africa must adopt a multi-faceted approach that addresses the various challenges and opportunities presented by the Middle East crisis. The following are five strategies that African countries can employ to achieve this objective:

### 1. Diversification of Investment Portfolios
One of the key strategies for protecting FDI gains is to diversify investment portfolios. This involves spreading investments across different sectors, countries, and asset classes to reduce exposure to any one particular market or industry. By doing so, African countries can minimize the risks associated with the Middle East crisis and ensure that their economies remain resilient in the face of external shocks. For example, countries such as Morocco and Tunisia have diversified their investment portfolios by investing in sectors such as renewable energy, tourism, and manufacturing.

### 2. Improvement of Business Environment
Another strategy for protecting and scaling FDI gains is to improve the business environment. This involves implementing policies and reforms that make it easier for foreign investors to operate in African countries. Examples of such policies include streamlining regulatory procedures, reducing bureaucratic hurdles, and improving infrastructure. By creating a more conducive business environment, African countries can attract more foreign investment and increase their competitiveness in the global economy. For instance, countries such as Rwanda and Mauritius have implemented business-friendly policies, such as one-stop shops for investors and streamlined tax regimes, to attract foreign investment.

### 3. Development of Infrastructure
The development of infrastructure is critical for attracting and retaining FDI. This includes investing in transportation networks, energy systems, and telecommunications infrastructure. By improving its infrastructure, Africa can reduce the costs and risks associated with doing business on the continent, making it a more attractive destination for foreign investors. For example, countries such as Egypt and South Africa have invested heavily in their transportation networks, including the construction of new airports, seaports, and highways.

### 4. Promotion of Regional Integration
Regional integration is another key strategy for protecting and scaling FDI gains. This involves promoting trade and investment between African countries, as well as with other regions. By doing so, Africa can create a larger and more integrated market, making it more attractive to foreign investors. For example, the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) is a regional integration initiative that aims to create a single market for goods and services across the continent. By promoting regional integration, African countries can increase their economic competitiveness and attract more foreign investment.

### 5. Enhancement of Investment Promotion Agencies
Finally, African countries can protect and scale their FDI gains by enhancing their investment promotion agencies (IPAs). IPAs play a critical role in attracting and retaining FDI, and their effectiveness can make a significant difference in a country's ability to attract foreign investment. By strengthening their IPAs, African countries can better promote their investment opportunities, provide support to investors, and facilitate the investment process. For example, countries such as South Africa and Nigeria have established strong IPAs that provide a range of services to investors, including investment facilitation, aftercare, and policy advocacy.

There are several examples of successful FDI strategies in Africa that can serve as models for other countries. For instance, the Ethiopian government has implemented a range of policies to attract FDI, including the establishment of industrial parks and the provision of tax incentives. As a result, Ethiopia has become a hub for textile manufacturing, with companies such as H&M and Calvin Klein setting up production facilities in the country. Similarly, the Rwandan government has implemented a range of policies to attract FDI in the technology sector, including the establishment of a technology park and the provision of tax incentives. As a result, Rwanda has become a leader in the technology sector, with companies such as Microsoft and Google investing in the country's burgeoning tech industry.

While Africa has made significant progress in attracting FDI, there are several mistakes that countries must avoid in order to protect and scale their gains. One of the key mistakes is to rely too heavily on a single sector or industry. For example, countries such as Angola and Nigeria have historically relied heavily on oil exports, making them vulnerable to fluctuations in global oil prices. By diversifying their economies and reducing their reliance on a single sector or industry, African countries can minimize their exposure to external shocks and ensure that their economies remain resilient.

Another mistake is to neglect the importance of good governance and institutional capacity. Foreign investors are often deterred by countries with weak institutions, corrupt practices, and poor governance. By strengthening their institutions and improving their governance, African countries can create a more attractive business environment and increase their competitiveness in the global economy.
